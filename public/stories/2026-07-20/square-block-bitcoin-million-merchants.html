A Million U.S. Stores Can Now Take Bitcoin

Block turned Lightning on across Square's terminals, waived the fees, and bolted on 5% bitcoin back — an attempt to buy a habit, not a headline.

By The Bitcoin Beacon · SAN FRANCISCO · July 20, 2026 · 5 min read
Lightning on the back end, a card-tap on the front — Illustration: The Bitcoin Beacon

The most consequential bitcoin-payments story of the year is not a new law or a nation-state reserve. It is a software update pushed to card readers already sitting on American countertops. When Block flipped bitcoin on across Square, it did not ask a million small businesses to buy anything, learn anything, or change how they ring up a sale.

That is the point. Square's rollout of native bitcoin acceptance — live to more than a million U.S. sellers, with full availability across roughly four million merchants the stated goal — is designed to make paying in bitcoin feel like paying with a card, because underneath it mostly is.

How it works at the counter

A customer can pay with a standard on-chain bitcoin wallet or over the Lightning Network. For most transactions the rail is Lightning, which settles in about a second for a trivial fee — the only version of bitcoin that makes sense for a $4 coffee. Block added tap-to-pay using the same NFC hardware merchants already use for cards, with Lightning processing invisibly on the back end.

The merchant chooses what to keep. A seller wary of bitcoin's swings can auto-convert every payment to dollars at the moment of sale, taking the settlement without the exposure. A seller who wants to stack sats can hold. Either way, the friction that killed a decade of “we accept bitcoin” window stickers — slow confirmations, high fees, price whiplash — is engineered out.

Buying the habit

Block is not leaving adoption to inertia. It waived processing fees on bitcoin payments through 2026, absorbing the cost to remove the last reason a small business might say no. Then it layered on a 5% “bitcoin back” rewards programme, paying consumers to pay in bitcoin at Square merchants.

Read together, those two moves are a deliberate attempt to manufacture a loop: a shopper earns bitcoin on a purchase, then spends it at the next participating store, which pays no fee to accept it. The company is spending real money to convert a novelty into a routine.

The friction that killed a decade of “we accept bitcoin” stickers — slow confirmations, high fees, price whiplash — is engineered out.

What it proves, and what it doesn't

The significance is scale. Circular economies in El Salvador, South Africa and Kenya proved bitcoin can be spent where the alternative is worse. Square proves it can be offered, in one rollout, to a chunk of the richest retail market on earth — a place where the alternative (a debit card) already works fine. That is a harder test of genuine demand.

Which is the honest caveat. Availability is not usage. Turning the feature on for a million merchants says nothing about how many customers actually pay in bitcoin rather than tapping the card they've used for years. The fee waiver expires; the 5% reward is an acquisition subsidy, not a permanent feature. The number that will matter next year is not merchants enabled but transactions completed — and Block has every incentive to publish the first and stay quiet about the second.

What Block switched on

  • Reach: native bitcoin acceptance live to 1M+ U.S. Square sellers; ~4M merchants the full-rollout target.
  • Rails: on-chain and Lightning, plus tap-to-pay over existing NFC hardware with Lightning on the back end.
  • Merchant choice: auto-convert each payment to dollars at checkout, or keep the bitcoin.
  • Incentives: processing fees waived through 2026; a 5% bitcoin-back rewards programme for shoppers.

The bottom line

Block just removed every technical excuse for a U.S. small business not to accept bitcoin. Whether Americans reach for it when a card is right there is the real experiment — and it's now running at a scale no circular economy could reach. Watch the transaction count, not the merchant count.
