Ghana Licenses the Firms Handling Its Bitcoin

A new law makes the Bank of Ghana the gatekeeper for exchanges and custodians — and eleven firms are already testing inside a regulated sandbox.

By The Bitcoin Beacon · ACCRA · July 20, 2026 · 5 min read
Accra's traders already move money by phone; now the rails have a rulebook — Illustration: The Bitcoin Beacon

For years, Ghanaians bought and sold bitcoin in a legal vacuum — not banned exactly, but not recognised, transacting through offshore apps the state could neither see nor tax. That era is ending. Ghana has a virtual-assets law, a designated regulator, and a live sandbox where the country's first licensed crypto firms are already operating.

Parliament passed the Virtual Asset Service Providers Bill in December 2025, and President John Dramani Mahama signed it into law before the year was out. It does not make bitcoin legal tender — the cedi keeps that title alone — but it does something more durable: it gives holding and trading bitcoin a legal home the government acknowledges.

One gatekeeper, many rails

The law names the Bank of Ghana as the primary licensing authority and stands up a Virtual Assets Regulatory Office inside the central bank. Every service provider — exchanges, wallet apps, custodians, payment platforms — must be licensed to operate. The Securities and Exchange Commission oversees trading and tokenisation, while the Financial Intelligence Centre handles the anti-money-laundering layer.

Implementation is already underway. The SEC opened a 12-month regulatory sandbox and admitted eleven firms to test services under supervision — a controlled environment where a company can operate for real customers while the regulator watches and writes the rulebook it will eventually enforce on everyone.

It does not make bitcoin legal tender — but it gives holding it a legal home the government acknowledges.

Why a mid-size economy bothered

Ghana's crypto market is estimated to move around $10 billion a year, most of it flowing through peer-to-peer channels and foreign platforms beyond any Ghanaian regulator's reach. A cedi that has lost ground against the dollar has done what weak currencies always do: pushed savers toward assets they can hold outside the local banking system.

Rather than fight that with a ban — the approach that failed next door in Nigeria for years — Accra chose to bring the activity onshore, where it can enforce standards, collect data, and eventually tax it. The Bank of Ghana has also floated a gold-backed stablecoin to shore up the cedi, a reminder that the state's enthusiasm is for control and revenue, not for bitcoin as such.

The catch

A framework on paper is not the same as a functioning market. Licensing regimes in the region have a habit of arriving with fees and capital requirements that squeeze out the small local operators who actually serve ordinary users, handing the market to a few well-capitalised foreign exchanges. The eleven sandbox firms are a start, not a verdict; what matters is whether a Ghanaian can still send bitcoin cheaply to a relative once the compliance costs land.

For now, the significance is directional. Another African government has concluded that the answer to bitcoin is not prohibition but a paper trail — a pattern the Beacon has traced this month from Zimbabwe to Nigeria, and now to Accra.

Ghana's VASP regime, in brief

  • The law: Virtual Asset Service Providers Bill passed December 2025, signed by President Mahama.
  • The regulator: Bank of Ghana is primary licensing authority via a new Virtual Assets Regulatory Office; SEC and FIC coordinate.
  • The sandbox: a 12-month SEC pilot with eleven firms testing licensed services.
  • Legal tender: unchanged — the cedi remains Ghana's only official currency.

The bottom line

Ghana didn't legalise bitcoin as money; it legalised the businesses that move it. That's the quieter, more common form of adoption — a state deciding it would rather license and watch the activity than pretend it isn't happening. The test now is whether the rules let small operators live, or hand the market to the giants.
