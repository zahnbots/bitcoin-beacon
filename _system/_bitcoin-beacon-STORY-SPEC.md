# The Bitcoin Beacon — Full Article Spec (website /stories pages)

The newsletter items stay SHORT (tease + link). The website articles they link to should be **substantial and specific** — longer because they carry more information, never because of filler.

## Length targets
- **Lead feature:** ~900–1,300 words.
- **Secondary stories:** ~600–900 words.
- Length is an OUTPUT of substance, not a goal. If you can't fill it with real information, it's shorter — don't pad.

## The anti-filler rule (the one that matters)
Every paragraph must add at least one NEW: fact, number, mechanism, quote, named entity, comparison, or perspective. If a sentence could be deleted without losing information, delete it. No restating the headline in different words; no throat-clearing; no "only time will tell."

## Depth levers — a MENU, not a checklist
Pick the **3–4 that genuinely serve THIS story** and let the rest go. Do NOT force all of them into every article — that's what makes a paper read formulaic. Vary which levers you use and in what order day to day. Only **Specifics** (#1) and accurate sourcing are constant; everything else is optional and situational.
1. **Specifics (near-always):** exact figures, dates, local-currency amounts, magnitudes, and a comparison that makes a number legible (e.g., "≈ powers 3,000 homes"). Cite each specific.
2. **Mechanism (use sparingly):** explain HOW it works only when the *how* is genuinely non-obvious or is the point of the story. Skip it when the mechanism is familiar (e.g., "another Lightning payment app") — a sentence will do.
3. **People & quotes:** a real name or attributed quote when one adds authority or color. Not mandatory.
4. **Context/history:** 1–3 sentences of how we got here, when the reader needs it to make sense of the news.
5. **Steelman / risk (use selectively):** air the strongest counterargument or real limits when the story is contested, hyped, or a genuine bet. Skip it for straightforward good/bad news — a forced "but critics say…" is filler.
6. **Second-order implications:** for the region, network, competitors, or policy — when there's a real "so what."
7. **On-the-ground color:** one concrete scene, person, or price. A little goes a long way.
8. **What to watch / open questions** at the end, when the thread is live.

Rule of thumb: a tight, specific 700-word piece that uses 3 levers well beats a 1,200-word piece that dutifully hits all 8.

## Structure — a flexible default, not a mold
Vary it story to story so the section doesn't read like a template. A good default: standfirst (italic deck) → dropcap intro grounded in a specific scene or fact → a few subhead sections, each advancing a DISTINCT point → an optional pull-quote and/or "by the numbers" box where they earn their place → an optional close ("the bottom line" / what to watch) → **Sources**.

What's CONSTANT: a specific, non-generic lead; sections that each add something new; and a real **Sources** list. What VARIES: section count, whether there's a pull-quote, a callout box, a steelman, a "bottom line," subhead style, and length. Some pieces are punchy 3-section reads; others are longer investigations. Mix it up on purpose — a reader flipping through five articles should not feel five identical skeletons.

## Accuracy (non-negotiable)
More detail = more citations. Every specific number, date, name, and quote must trace to a source in the Sources list. If a specific can't be verified, don't invent it — either omit it or clearly mark it as an estimate/range with the source. Verify titles/roles before quoting anyone.
