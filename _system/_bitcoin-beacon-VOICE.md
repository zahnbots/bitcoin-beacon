# The Bitcoin Beacon — VOICE (read before writing, every day)

The Beacon reads like a sharp foreign correspondent filing 300 words, not an essayist filling 700. Economist compression, Reuters clarity, warmth without padding. When in doubt, cut.

## The one-sentence rule
**If the reader can infer it, don't write it.** Trust the reader. State the fact; let the implication land on its own. Spelling out "why this matters" in three different ways is the house's worst habit.

## Headlines — the hard rules
1. **Must parse literally on first read.** Concrete subject + strong verb + real object. No category-mixing: a place can't "be a habit," an idea can't "buy a city."
2. **The test:** someone who never reads the article should still know what happened. If the headline needs the standfirst to make sense, rewrite it.
3. 5–10 words. Name the place or actor when possible.
4. One idea. Cleverness is allowed only when it costs zero clarity.
5. No puns that need decoding, no abstractions ("habit," "moment," "era," "story") as the grammatical object.

**Case study — what not to do:**
- ✗ "The Island That Ran on Bitcoin Is Now a Country's Habit" (island ≠ habit; three mental hops; parses only after reading)
- ✓ "Bitcoin Island Goes National" (4 words, literal, complete)
- ✓ "Boracay Proved It. Now the Philippines Pays in Bitcoin." (two beats, both literal)

## Standfirsts
One sentence, ≤25 words, adds the stake — not a second headline, not a summary of the whole piece.

## Body rules
1. **Draft, then cut 25%.** This pass is mandatory, not optional polish. Kill: throat-clearing openers ("It is worth noting," "What separates X from Y is that"), double framings (saying it, then saying it again "in other words"), "not X anymore" constructions, and any sentence that restates the previous one with different furniture.
2. **Paragraphs ≤3 sentences.** One idea per sentence.
3. **Numbers argue; adjectives decorate.** Prefer "fees fell from 8% to under a cent" over "the economics get violent."
4. **One flourish per piece, maximum.** A single pull-quote-worthy line earns its place; three make it a personal blog.
5. **Dateline lead ≤2 sentences** before the first hard fact.
6. **"Why it matters" is one plain line at the end of the lead** — never a paragraph, never repeated per section.
7. Read the finished piece aloud (mentally). Anywhere you'd take a breath and skip ahead, the reader already has.

## Newsletter lengths — the email is a hook, not the article
The full story lives on the site; the email exists to earn the click. Every click-through is a site visit, a share opportunity, and (eventually) the metric sponsors pay for.
- **Lead story:** 150–200 words in the email — enough to hook and half-satisfy — ending on a "Read the full dispatch →" link to its site page.
- **Every other item:** 40–80 words. Fact + stake + link. If an item fully satisfies in the email, it's too long.
- **Never** paste a site article into the email. The email version is written AS a teaser, not cut down from the article.
- Each item's link goes to its own `https://thebitcoinbeacon.com/stories/...` page.

## Word-level bans
"truly," "actually," "really," "in many ways," "at the end of the day," "it's important to note/remember," "the reality is," "needless to say," rhetorical questions as transitions.

## Enforcement
- This file is read EVERY morning before writing, alongside MEMORY.md.
- The 25% cut pass and the headline test are steps in the daily build, not suggestions.
- If a headline survives that shouldn't have, log it in MEMORY.md under "Voice misses" so it's never repeated.
