Bolivia Ran Out of Dollars. Bitcoin Filled the Gap.

A currency crisis drained the country of hard cash — and pushed La Paz vendors, importers and even the state energy firm onto bitcoin’s rails.

LA PAZ — When the boliviano’s dollar peg cracked and the banks ran dry, Bolivians did not wait for a rescue. They opened wallets. Virtual-asset transactions ran about $294 million in the first half of 2025, up from $46.5 million a year earlier — a jump of roughly 532%.

Most of that flows through dollar-pegged stablecoins. But the bitcoin layer is where the everyday spending lives: a La Paz coffee stand takes payment through a Blink Lightning QR; barbershops and gyms quote in satoshis; a national QR standard, OpenBCB, went live to crypto wallets via AEON on July 9, settling merchants back into bolivianos. Even the state energy firm, YPFB, has used crypto to pay for fuel imports it could not source dollars for.

Why it matters: when the dollars vanish, bitcoin is the rail with no correspondent bank left to say no.

The Take · Opinion

America Banned a Digital Dollar It Never Built

A housing bill became law on July 10 without a signature, and buried inside it is a permanent ban on a Federal Reserve digital currency through 2030. Same week, Circle won a national trust-bank charter to custody the dollars behind its stablecoin.

Washington did not choose between a state digital dollar and cash. It chose between a state digital dollar and a private one — and picked private. The rails to a programmable dollar now belong to companies, under bank charters, with reversibility built in.

That leaves exactly one digital money no institution issues, freezes, or prints: bitcoin. The CBDC fight was never bitcoin’s to win. Its edge is being the only option on the table that answers to no one.
