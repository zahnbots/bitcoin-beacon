Thailand Lets Tourists Spend Bitcoin in Baht

A government sandbox turns a visitor’s bitcoin into baht at the checkout — no bank account, no legal-tender law, just a QR code.

BANGKOK — At a night-market stall the sale looks ordinary: a phone, a QR code, a chime in baht. What the vendor never sees is that the money started as a tourist’s bitcoin. That is the whole design of TouristDigiPay, Thailand’s crypto-to-baht sandbox — an 18-month government pilot that sends a visitor’s bitcoin to a licensed exchange, converts it to baht, and loads a Tourist Wallet for QR payments.

Merchants receive only baht; approved shops can take up to ฿500,000 a month, small vendors ฿50,000; cash withdrawals are banned. Oversight is shared by the finance ministry, the SEC, the Bank of Thailand and the anti-money-laundering office. Bangkok hopes it lifts visitor spending around 10%, up to ฿175 billion (~$15bn).

It is the Boracay pattern from the top down: no legal-tender law, just a checkout that finally accepts what travelers already hold. The catch is in the plumbing — a licensed operator sells the bitcoin and settles fiat, so no one truly pays in bitcoin, and stablecoins may carry most of the volume.

Why it matters: adoption tends to arrive as a better checkout, not a mandate.

Network & Mining · Addis Ababa

Ethiopia wants to own its bitcoin mines, not just host them. At Finance Forward Ethiopia 2026, PM Abiy Ahmed said the state fund is seeking mining partners; a February $250M tie-up gives it a 20–30% stake in new facilities. Africa’s biggest miner (~2.5% of global hashrate) wants a share of the rigs, not just the power bill. Full story →

Policy & Nation-States · Abuja

Nigeria opens a legal door for its bitcoin traders. Five years after the central bank cut crypto off from the banks, the SEC’s incubation programme now supervises nine firms — Luno was the first global exchange admitted, joined this month by Kucoin and GIGX. A licensed onshore ramp for the world’s biggest grassroots market; stablecoins still dominate the volume. Full story →

Money & Macro · Kyiv

Ukraine weighs putting bitcoin in its war chest. A cross-party draft (No. 13356) would let the National Bank hold bitcoin in official reserves — permissive, not compulsory. The logic is written in 2022’s freeze of Russia’s reserves: a bearer asset no adversary can switch off. Early-stage, no timetable, Binance reportedly assisting. Full story →

The Take

The Bitcoin Treasury Boom Is Splitting in Two

One line of bitcoin-holding companies still raises hundreds of millions on demand — Treasury B.V. just pulled in $147M from Winklevoss Capital and Nakamoto for a Euronext listing. The other can’t fill a rights issue at a 10% yield: Sweden’s preferred-share raise closed 52% subscribed, and Adam Back’s BSTR saw its SPAC listing collapse this month.

The dividing line is the premium. When shares trade above the coins, a treasury issues stock and buys more; when they sink to coin value, the flywheel jams. Survivors have brand, cheap capital, or real cash flow (see Salinas). The shakeout isn’t the model breaking — it’s the model finally being priced.
