Vietnam Makes Bitcoin Property, Not Money

Eighteen million Vietnamese own bitcoin. Since January they own it lawfully — and still cannot spend it.

HANOI — Vietnam spent a decade pretending its bitcoin owners did not exist. On January 1 the pretence ended: the Law on Digital Technology Industry took effect and bitcoin became property under Vietnamese civil law — ownable, inheritable, defensible in court.

Roughly 18 million Vietnamese hold digital assets, and the OECD’s 2026 Asia Capital Markets Report puts the country’s inflows at about 55% of GDP. All of that was true before the law. The law only stopped the state from having to look away.

What it did not do is make bitcoin money. Payment stays prohibited; the dong keeps its monopoly. Beside the property law sits Resolution 05/2025, a five-year pilot licensing regime for exchanges with terms that read like a moat: VND 10 trillion (about $400 million) of charter capital, 65% institutional ownership, foreign stakes capped at 49%.

Why it matters: Vietnam has legalised holding bitcoin and kept spending it illegal — a bet that property rights alone will pull the market out of the shadows.

The Take · Opinion

JPMorgan Says Private Blockchains Are Bitcoin’s Real Threat

The bank’s analysts call Strategy’s stack a volatility story, not an existential one. What should worry bitcoin, they argue, is the $4 trillion already moving across JPMorgan’s own Kinexys network and the fifteen-plus banks building tokenised finance on permissioned rails that never touch a public chain.

They are right about the flows and wrong about the contest. A permissioned ledger is a faster database with a bank’s promise attached. Bitcoin sells the one thing a bank cannot issue about itself: settlement nobody can reverse. Those are different products, and only one of them competes with the bank.
