Zimbabwe Puts Its Bitcoin Economy on the Books

HARARE — On Harare’s pavements the exchange rate that matters isn’t the central bank’s: dollars in, bitcoin or a dollar token out, settled before the customer crosses the street. This month the trade got a rulebook. Statutory Instrument 99 of 2026 — Zimbabwe’s first virtual-asset framework — makes anyone who buys, sells, moves or stores crypto register with the Reserve Bank’s financial-intelligence unit, binding since June 16. The price of entry: US$500 a year, a 2% transaction levy, a 15% withholding tax on cross-border digital services. It’s a sharp reversal for a state that in 2018 ordered banks to shun crypto — and never stopped the peer-to-peer trade that followed. Zimbabweans have buried two currencies in two decades; bitcoin and dollar stablecoins are how they keep this week’s earnings until next week.

Why it matters: when a government stops banning a money and starts taxing it, the argument is over.
