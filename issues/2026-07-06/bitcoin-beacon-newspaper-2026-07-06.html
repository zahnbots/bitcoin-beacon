Bitcoin Island Goes National

Boracay proved tourists would pay in bitcoin. Now 400-plus Philippine merchants take it — and the real prize is a $35-billion remittance market.

BORACAY, PHILIPPINES — Four years ago a single wallet company decided this four-kilometre resort island would learn to spend bitcoin. Today the experiment has outgrown its beach. Pouch.ph, the firm behind the “Bitcoin Island” project, says more than 400 merchants across the country now accept bitcoin payments — upward of 250 of them on Boracay itself, with the rest spreading into Cebu City, Iloilo, Dumaguete and Bacolod. Convenience stores, dive shops, hotels and carinderias take a phone tap and settle in seconds over the Lightning Network for a fraction of a cent.

What makes the Philippine story different from El Salvador’s is that nobody legislated it. There is no legal-tender mandate, no state wallet, no top-down decree. Adoption arrived through two of the most practical doors imaginable: tourism and remittances. Foreign visitors who already hold bitcoin can spend it directly; merchants who want that traffic learn to accept it. The rails did the persuading.

The bigger prize sits offshore. The Philippines is one of the largest remittance markets on earth, leaning on more than $35 billion a year sent home from abroad — over $12 billion of it from the United States alone. Strike’s “Send Globally” product, built with Pouch as its local partner, converts a sender’s dollars into bitcoin, moves them over Lightning, and drops pesos into a recipient’s bank or mobile-money account. Neither side ever has to think about custody or price. By April 2026 Strike said it was clearing more than two million Lightning transactions a month.

That is the quiet mechanism reshaping cross-border money: Lightning can cut the 6–10% that legacy remittance firms charge down to rounding-error territory, beating even the UN’s 3% target by a wide margin. For a family receiving a monthly transfer, the difference is a week of groceries.

WHY IT MATTERS. The Philippines is quietly writing the playbook other remittance economies will copy: you don’t need citizens to hold bitcoin, only to let it travel underneath the money they already use. Adoption that arrives as cheaper plumbing, not ideology, is the kind that sticks.

The Take · Opinion

A Bitcoin Miner Keeps the Lights On in Malawi

The tired argument about bitcoin’s energy use assumes mining competes with people for power. In much of the Global South the opposite is true: mining is the reason new power gets built at all. Gridless, the Block-backed miner, now runs seven sites across Kenya, Malawi and Zambia, parking containers of machines next to small run-of-river hydro plants that no utility would finance on their own.

In Bondo, Malawi, that arrangement now keeps the lights on for more than 1,800 households far from any national grid. The miner is the anchor tenant — the guaranteed buyer that makes a village micro-hydro station bankable — and the surplus electrifies homes, clinics and phone chargers. Gridless is now raising to build its own run-of-river projects with mining baked in from day one.

Strip away the price charts and this is what “bitcoin adoption” looks like at the frontier: not a speculative wallet, but a child reading after dark. Any energy debate that ignores that is having the wrong argument.

— The Bitcoin Beacon, opinion
On the Ground · India

MUMBAI. India keeps buying bitcoin the hard way. Despite a punishing 30% tax on gains and a 1% withholding levy on every transfer, the country again ranks first in grassroots crypto activity in the latest global adoption index, with tier-2, -3 and -4 cities driving roughly 75% of the volume. From April 1, exchanges must report user transactions straight to the Income Tax Department — a clampdown that has pushed some traders offshore but has not dented demand. Officials have also floated exploring a national bitcoin reserve held “like gold,” short of legal tender.

Money & Macro · Reserves

WASHINGTON. The United States is moving to put its bitcoin reserve in writing. The American Reserve Modernization Act of 2026 (H.R. 8957), from Reps. Nick Begich and Jared Golden, was published in full in May: it would house a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve inside the Treasury, seeded from coins forfeited to the government, with a 20-year lock-up, quarterly cryptographic “proof-of-reserve” attestations and independent audits. Patrick Witt, executive director of the President’s Council of Advisers for Digital Assets, told a Miami audience that a reserve announcement is coming “in the next few weeks.”

Policy & Nation-States · Bhutan

THIMPHU. Bhutan, long celebrated for quietly mining a sovereign bitcoin fortune on hydropower, is now quietly spending it. On-chain trackers flagged a 738-BTC transfer worth about $44.9 million on June 6; by analysts’ count the kingdom has now sold roughly 70% of the ~13,000 BTC it held in late 2024, down to about 3,954 BTC (~$280 million), with its mining apparently halted since around November 2024. The proceeds are widely tied to funding the Gelephu Mindfulness City megaproject. State fund DHI disputes the reads: chief executive Ujjwal Deep Dahal says he “doesn’t recall” recent sales.

Markets & Institutions

NEW YORK. The two big pools of institutional bitcoin are pulling in opposite directions. Public companies now hold a record of more than 750,000 BTC between them — Strategy alone sits above 580,000 — as the treasury playbook spreads. Yet US spot bitcoin ETFs just logged a sixth straight week of net outflows, roughly $5.94 billion drained cumulatively. Read together, it is long-horizon balance-sheet buyers accumulating while faster ETF money rotates out — the same coin, two very different clocks.

On the Ground · Argentina

BUENOS AIRES. Argentina’s central bank is clearing the country’s banks to offer bitcoin and crypto services this year, a policy reversal that would put custody and trading in front of ordinary savers who have spent a decade fleeing a collapsing peso. Adoption already runs near 20% of the population — the highest in Latin America — and pre-loaded cards let holders spend while merchants still receive pesos. For a country where inflation has repeatedly topped triple digits, bank-grade bitcoin access is less a novelty than a life raft.
