South Korea Moves to Treat Bitcoin as a National Asset

A Blue House briefing folded digital assets into the law that governs state wealth — and cleared a path to the country’s first spot bitcoin ETFs.

SEOUL — South Korea’s finance ministry told reporters at the presidential office on July 15 that it will write virtual assets into the way the state itself is run. A planned National Asset Basic Act would replace a 1950s property statute and, for the first time, list crypto alongside intellectual property as a category of national asset the government must formally manage.

In the same breath, officials said they will amend the Capital Markets Act to permit spot digital-asset ETFs, with bitcoin products first in line, and press ahead with a broader Digital Asset Basic Act in the second half of the year. Corporations were already cleared in January to hold bitcoin on their balance sheets, capped at 5% of equity a year.

Why it matters: one of the world’s largest economies is deciding bitcoin belongs inside the machinery of public finance, not outside it.

The Take

The Bitcoin ETFs Are Bleeding. The Price Barely Moved.

US spot funds lost $425 million in a single day this week and roughly $5.8 billion so far this year. In a market that once treated flows as gospel, that should have been a rout. Bitcoin instead held near $63,000.

The reason is who is on each side. The sellers are paper — leveraged traders and rotating institutions. On the other side, long-term holders now sit on some 84% of the supply and are not for sale. When outflows meet coins that never come to market, the number stops meaning what Wall Street thinks it means.

On the Ground

MANILA. Filipinos can now pay with bitcoin at roughly 700,000 shops. The wallet Coins.ph plugged bitcoin into QR Ph, the central bank’s national QR standard, converting it to pesos at the register so the merchant carries no volatility. It rides a remittance economy worth more than $35 billion a year, where Lightning transfers already undercut the 6–10% that traditional remitters skim. Full story →

Network & Mining

VIENTIANE. Laos is pulling the plug on the miners it once courted. The “battery of Southeast Asia” let bitcoin operations soak up surplus hydropower; last year they ate more than a third of the country’s output and helped drive a 50% jump in demand. Now, buried under roughly $1.3 billion a year in debt service, Vientiane says it will cut miners off and steer the power to industry and AI. Full story →

Money & Macro

BRASÍLIA. Brazil has handed its bitcoin businesses a rulebook and a clock. Central-bank resolutions that took force in February require custodians to segregate client coins, publish monthly proof of reserves and submit to biennial audits — and give firms already operating until October 30 to comply or leave. Brazil handles close to a third of Latin America’s crypto volume, so the cull will reshape the region’s largest market. Full story →

Markets & Institutions

NEW YORK. Two of the West’s biggest banks are about to hold bitcoin for clients. Citigroup plans to launch institutional bitcoin custody this year, folding the asset into the same safekeeping, reporting and tax rails it uses for stocks and bonds; Germany’s Deutsche Bank is building its own, using Bitpanda and Taurus infrastructure. Both are institutional-only at the start. Full story →
