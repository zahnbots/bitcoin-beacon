Lebanon’s Banks Froze. Bitcoin Keeps Moving.

A collapsed banking system taught Lebanese to hold value on their phones — and bitcoin is the piece no bank can freeze.

BEIRUT — At a Beirut fruit stall the price is quoted in lira but settled on a phone. Six years after Lebanon’s banks froze deposits and the lira lost most of its value, the country runs on money people can hold themselves — dollars in hand, and increasingly bitcoin and dollar-stablecoins on a screen.

Surveys put crypto-wallet use near a fifth of the population, among the highest rates anywhere, and the driver is not speculation but a broken banking system. Aid groups have piloted stablecoin payments that skip the banks entirely; merchants price in dollars and take whatever settles fastest.

Bitcoin is the smaller share of the volume — the tokenized dollar still wins the day-to-day — but it is the one instrument no government issues and no bank can freeze.

Why it matters: where the banks stopped working, Lebanese didn’t wait for a law to make bitcoin money.

The Take · Opinion

Forty Countries Pledged Bitcoin. Thirteen Actually Hold It.

More than 40 governments have “committed” to bitcoin, by one industry count. Public trackers show 13 with confirmed holdings, worth about $37.9B — and most of that is seized coin, not bought. The U.S. sits on ~328,000 BTC it forfeited, with no purchase program. El Salvador’s buying is paused; Bhutan is drawing its stack down; Pakistan’s “national wallet” met a central bank that still calls crypto banned.

A pledge is a press release. A balance sheet is a decision. So far the gap between them is the whole story.

Policy & Nation-States

PANAMA CITY. A bill before Panama’s National Assembly would recognize bitcoin, stablecoins and other digital assets as a lawful means of payment — when both parties agree — while licensing exchanges under the banking and financial-intelligence regulators. It is El Salvador’s idea without the mandate: no one must take bitcoin, but the law would stop pretending no one does. A president vetoed a similar law in 2022.

Markets & Institutions

STOCKHOLM. Bitcoin Treasury Capital lists BTC PREF, billed as Sweden’s first bitcoin-backed preferred share, on July 20 — carrying a 10% annual dividend paid monthly. The catch: the rights issue behind it closed at 52% subscription, raising about SEK 12.2M ($1.3M) of a SEK 23.4M target. Even a double-digit yield couldn’t fill the book, a cool read on Europe’s appetite for leveraged bitcoin equity.

Money & Macro

CAIRO. Egypt’s central bank is building a digital pound — and says one reason is to pull Egyptians off the crypto its own currency crisis pushed them toward. The pound was floated in 2024 and lost about 40%; roughly 3 million Egyptians hold crypto wallets, trading peer-to-peer around a ban. The e-pound is a proof-of-concept aimed at 2030. Bitcoin needs no pilot.

Network & Mining

MUSCAT. Oman is turning waste into hashrate. The Gulf sultanate is licensing regulated miners to run on flared gas, spare grid power and solar — with KYC checks, local vendors and tech jobs attached — and has edged into the global top tier of mining hosts. It is the mirror image of Laos and Paraguay: instead of pricing miners out, a petro-state is inviting them in to monetize gas it would otherwise burn for nothing.
