Lebanon's Banks Froze. Bitcoin Keeps Moving.

BEIRUT — At a Beirut fruit stall the price is quoted in lira but settled on a phone. Six years after Lebanon's banks froze deposits and the lira lost most of its value, the country runs on money people can hold themselves — dollars in hand, and increasingly bitcoin and dollar-stablecoins on a screen.

Surveys put crypto-wallet use near a fifth of the population, among the highest rates anywhere, and the driver is not speculation but a broken banking system. Aid groups have piloted stablecoin payments that skip the banks entirely; merchants price in dollars and take whatever settles fastest. Bitcoin is the smaller share of the volume — the tokenized dollar still wins the day-to-day — but it is the one instrument no government issues and no bank can freeze.

Why it matters: where the banks stopped working, Lebanese didn't wait for a law to make bitcoin money.
