A $50 Transfer to Lagos Now Costs Two Cents

A fee that legacy remitters can’t follow down — and the killer app for bitcoin in Africa was never the price chart.

LAGOS — Send $50 to a family member in Lagos through a traditional remitter and the fee is roughly $3.25. Send the same $50 over the Lightning Network through Bitnob and it lands in seconds for a cent or two. That gap is the whole story of bitcoin’s quiet spread across Africa.

Bitnob, founded in 2020 and built for the continent’s hardest payment corridors, now moves payroll for remote workers across 23 African countries, with transaction volume up 340% year on year and more than $4.5 billion routed through its rails. Its Strike partnership lets someone in the United States send dollars that arrive as local currency in an African bank account — bitcoin the invisible wire in between, never touched by either end.

In June it launched Bitnob Enterprise, selling that infrastructure to banks and fintechs.

Why it matters: adoption in the Global South follows the cheapest way to get paid, not the loudest headline.

The Take

States Mine Bitcoin Before They Trust You to Hold It

Kazakhstan will stock a national reserve from its miners. Paraguay sells them the cheapest power on the continent. Pakistan earmarked 2,000 megawatts; Ethiopia hosts state-owned rigs. Yet several of these same governments still make it hard for an ordinary citizen to own a single coin. The pattern is consistent: states are comfortable with bitcoin as a revenue line they control, and wary of it as money they can’t. Mining is a tax on electricity they already run; self-custody is a person they can’t. Revenue is easy. Letting go is the hard part.

Policy & Nation-States

ASTANA. Kazakhstan approved rules to seed a national crypto reserve from its bitcoin miners. A presidential decree (July 7) and Resolution No. 638 (July 18, effective August 1) give large licensed miners capped-tariff power on 10-year contracts; in return they hand a slice of output monthly to a state fund managed by the national bank. The government accumulates bitcoin without buying a coin. Full story →

Money & Macro

BRASÍLIA. From July 1, Brazil’s tax office runs DeCripto: anyone trading bitcoin on foreign exchanges, DeFi, or peer-to-peer above R$35,000 a month must file every month, aligned to the OECD’s reporting standard. Gains are taxed at a flat 17.5%. Domestic exchanges already report for you. The paper trail tightens as adoption climbs. Full story →

Network & Mining

ASUNCIÓN. Paraguay’s state utility expects mining revenue to more than triple, from $100 million in 2024 to about $350 million in 2026 — even as the number of legal operators roughly halves. A handful of large firms have locked up most of the 943 megawatts of contracted capacity, fed by surplus power from the Itaipú dam at roughly $0.033 a kilowatt-hour. Full story →
