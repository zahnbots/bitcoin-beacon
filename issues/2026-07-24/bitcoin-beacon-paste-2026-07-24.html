A $50 Transfer to Lagos Now Costs Two Cents

LAGOS, Nigeria — Send $50 to family in Lagos through a traditional remitter and the fee is around $3.25. Send it over the Lightning Network through Bitnob and it lands in seconds for a cent or two. That gap is the whole story of bitcoin’s quiet spread across Africa.

Bitnob, founded in 2020 for the continent’s hardest payment corridors, now moves payroll for remote workers across 23 African countries, with volume up 340% year on year and more than $4.5 billion routed through its rails. Its Strike partnership lets someone in the U.S. send dollars that arrive as local currency in an African bank account — bitcoin the invisible wire in between, never touched by either end. In June it launched Bitnob Enterprise, selling that infrastructure to banks and fintechs.

Why it matters: adoption in the Global South follows the cheapest way to get paid, not the loudest headline.

Kazakhstan Will Fill a State Bitcoin Reserve From Its Miners

A presidential decree and Resolution No. 638 (effective August 1) give large licensed miners capped-tariff power on 10-year deals; in return they hand a slice of output monthly to a state fund run by the national bank. The government stacks bitcoin without buying a coin. Full story →

Brazil Tells Bitcoin Holders to Report Every Month

From July 1, Brazilians trading bitcoin on foreign exchanges, DeFi, or peer-to-peer above R$35,000 a month must file monthly under a new system called DeCripto, aligned to the OECD standard. Gains are taxed at a flat 17.5%. Full story →

Paraguay Earns More From Bitcoin, With Fewer Miners

State power sales to bitcoin miners are set to more than triple to ~$350 million as small operators fold and a few giants lock up the Itaipú dam’s cheap electricity at about $0.033 a kilowatt-hour. Full story →
