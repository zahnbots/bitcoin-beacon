A Senegalese Startup Wires Bitcoin Into Mobile Money

Banxaas now lets West Africans swap between the CFA franc and bitcoin inside the mobile-money apps they already use — across four countries.

DAKAR — In the markets of Senegal’s capital, the money that clears fastest now rides two rails at once: mobile money on top, bitcoin underneath. Banxaas, built by the Senegalese startup Yité Technologies, lets people swap between the CFA franc and bitcoin directly inside the mobile-money wallets they already use — Wave, Orange Money, MTN and others — and now reaches four countries: Senegal, Côte d’Ivoire, Guinea and Cameroon.

The pitch is not speculation. It is settlement. A trader in Dakar can be paid by a supplier in Abidjan, or by a relative abroad, and receive francs on a phone in seconds, with bitcoin’s Lightning Network doing the cross-border leg the banks either overcharge for or refuse. In June, a tool built on Banxaas won first place at the bitcoin++ hackathon in Nairobi for stitching West African mobile money to Kenya’s M-Pesa.

Why it matters: francophone West Africa just got a bitcoin rail wired into the apps people already trust — no bank, no branch, no wait.

Policy & Nation-States

RABAT. Morocco is moving to end a ban that never worked. Bill 42.25, drafted by the central bank with the finance ministry and markets regulator, would license exchanges and pull bitcoin under formal supervision — reversing a 2017 prohibition that pushed one of Africa’s busiest peer-to-peer markets underground rather than closing it. Parliamentary review is underway; officials have floated adoption as soon as this year. Full story →

Markets & Institutions

TOKYO. Metaplanet, Japan’s largest corporate bitcoin holder at roughly 43,000 BTC, has bought a licensed securities firm and renamed it Metaplanet Securities. The plan — Project Nova — is to issue bitcoin-backed bonds and build digital-credit products with a yen-stablecoin issuer and Japan’s Progmat platform, using the treasury as collateral rather than just holding it. A treasury company is becoming a lender. Full story →

Money & Macro

NEW DELHI. India taxes bitcoin at 30% on gains plus a 1% levy on every trade, and this year added penalties for exchanges that file late. Yet the Reserve Bank of India still tells the government it wants the asset banned outright, per papers reported by Reuters. The state collects on a market its own central bank refuses to bless — while some ruling-party voices float a national reserve. Full story →

The Take · Opinion

The Miners Are Leaving. Bitcoin Doesn’t Mind.

Hashrate has fallen roughly 15% since January as miners divert megawatts to AI, and difficulty has stepped down through a dozen adjustments this year. Read as a health chart, that looks like decline. It isn’t.

Difficulty is the network’s thermostat: when miners leave, mining gets cheaper for whoever stays, and blocks keep arriving about every ten minutes no matter how many machines are plugged in. The security budget floats; the issuance schedule doesn’t. A network that shrugs off indifference is doing exactly what it was built to do.
