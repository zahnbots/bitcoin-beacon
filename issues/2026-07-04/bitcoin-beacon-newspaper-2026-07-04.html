NAIROBI — In Kibera, the city’s largest informal settlement, roughly 200 residents now buy vegetables, water and boda-boda rides with bitcoin over the Lightning Network — each payment settling in under a second for a fraction of a cent, no bank account required. The grassroots group Afribit Africa has onboarded 150-plus merchants and logged more than 2,000 transactions since 2022.

What turns a pilot into a market is reach. In May, the Tando app began converting Kenyans’ M-Pesa phone numbers directly into Lightning addresses — meaning roughly 40 million people, effectively the country’s entire adult population, can now receive a bitcoin payment without downloading anything new. Feature-phone users aren’t left behind either: Machankura’s USSD service already spans ten African countries and 39,000-plus handsets, no smartphone or internet needed.

This is the adoption that actually matters — and it looks nothing like the version the West measures. While New York argues over ETF flows and treasury discounts, a fruit seller in Kibera closing a sale over Lightning is the product working exactly as designed. Money is a network, and networks grow from the edges inward, not from a Wall Street press release outward.

The Global South is bitcoin’s real total addressable market: high inflation, thin banking, mobile-first, punishing remittance fees. That is precisely where a bearer asset that settles instantly for almost nothing stops being a speculation and becomes an upgrade. WHY IT MATTERS. The number worth tracking isn’t the price — it’s that, as of May, “can someone without a bank receive value instantly for almost nothing?” quietly became yes for 40 million Kenyans. That is the monetary-network thesis being proven in real time.

On the Ground

SAN SALVADOR — El Zonte’s original circular economy grinds on: residents pay utilities, medical care, food and haircuts over Lightning, and new businesses opt in each month — now entirely bottom-up and voluntary as the government sunsets the state Chivo wallet under its IMF pact. Adoption here no longer needs a mandate.

REMITTANCES. El Salvador’s crypto-linked remittances jumped ~50% year-over-year in Q1 to $17.38M — real growth, but still just 0.71% of the country’s $2.43B total. The rail is far cheaper (Lightning under a cent vs. 6–10% legacy fees); habit and cash-out points are what still gate it. An honest picture beats a triumphant one.

The Take · Opinion

In the Global South, bitcoin’s real rival isn’t the dollar — it’s the CBDC

Every central bank watching grassroots bitcoin spread through its economy is reaching for the same counter: a central bank digital currency, marketed as “financial inclusion” with the state’s hand left firmly on the switch.

But the two offer opposite bargains. A CBDC is programmable, surveillable and freezable by design — inclusion on a leash. Bitcoin is a bearer instrument that answers to no issuer. For populations scarred by devaluation and capital controls, that gap isn’t academic; it’s the entire point — and it is why top-down digital-currency pilots keep stalling while bottom-up bitcoin quietly compounds.

The developing world’s real monetary contest this decade won’t be bitcoin versus the dollar. It’ll be open money versus programmable money — and on the street, the side that respects the person holding it tends to win.

— The Bitcoin Beacon
