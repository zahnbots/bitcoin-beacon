In Kibera, Cleaning the Streets Pays in Bitcoin

In Nairobi’s largest settlement, young people earn sats for collecting waste — and spend them at the shops next door.

NAIROBI — In Kibera, one of Africa’s largest informal settlements, a bag of collected plastic now converts into money the collector controls. Afribit Africa pays young people in satoshis — the smallest units of bitcoin — for community waste work, then signs up the shops around them to accept it.

Roughly 2,600 residents are inside the loop: earning bitcoin on weekends, spending it at grocers, kiosks and vegetable stalls that settle over the Lightning Network through Blink wallets. No bank account is required, and no bank takes a cut. The money moves phone-to-phone for a fraction of a cent, in a neighbourhood formal finance never bothered to reach.

It is small, and deliberately so. A circular economy holds only if the same coins keep moving between the same hands — earned, spent, re-earned — instead of leaking out as charity. Kibera is a test of whether that loop survives contact with a place the banking system forgot.

Why it matters: bitcoin’s clearest use isn’t a corporate treasury — it’s a wallet nobody can freeze.

The Take

Let the Stablecoins Have the Checkout

When Bolivia moved to bless the dollar-pegged USDT for everyday payments this month, bitcoiners winced. They shouldn’t. A coin that swings 5% before lunch was never going to price a loaf of bread, and pretending otherwise has cost the movement credibility for a decade.

Bitcoin’s edge isn’t stability — it’s that no one can print it, seize it, or switch it off. That makes it a savings technology first and a payment rail second. Let dollar tokens win the corner-store tap. The moment those dollars need to escape the country, the issuer, or inflation, they meet the one asset with no counterparty. Losing the checkout isn’t a defeat. It’s a division of labour.

Network & Mining

ISLAMABAD. Pakistan has made its crypto regulator permanent. The Virtual Assets Act 2026 turned the Pakistan Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority into a standing federal body that licenses exchanges and custodians — Bitget is among the first cleared to operate. Alongside it runs an industrial bet: 2,000 megawatts of surplus power earmarked for bitcoin mining and data centres, with miner fees feeding a state reserve. Full story →

Markets & Institutions

SAN FRANCISCO. Block has switched bitcoin on across Square. More than a million U.S. merchants can now take Lightning payments through hardware they already own; Block waived the processing fees through 2026 and layered on 5% bitcoin back. Tap-to-pay runs Lightning on the back end, so paying in bitcoin feels like tapping a card. The pitch isn’t novelty — it’s habit. Full story →

Policy & Nation-States

ACCRA. Ghana has put its bitcoin economy under one roof. A new Virtual Asset Service Providers law makes the Bank of Ghana the licensing authority for exchanges, custodians and wallet apps, and eleven firms are already trading inside a 12-month regulated sandbox. The cedi stays the only legal tender — but holding bitcoin here now leaves a paper trail the state recognises. Full story →
