In Kibera, Cleaning the Streets Pays in Bitcoin

NAIROBI, Kenya — In Kibera, one of Africa's largest informal settlements, a bag of collected plastic now converts into money the collector controls. Afribit Africa pays young people in satoshis — the smallest units of bitcoin — for community waste work, then signs up the shops around them to accept it.

Roughly 2,600 residents are inside the loop: earning bitcoin on weekends, spending it at grocers, kiosks and vegetable stalls that settle over the Lightning Network through Blink wallets. No bank account, no bank cut — the money moves phone-to-phone for a fraction of a cent, in a neighbourhood formal finance never bothered to reach.

It is small, and deliberately so. A circular economy holds only if the same coins keep moving between the same hands — earned, spent, re-earned — instead of leaking out as charity.

Why it matters: bitcoin's clearest use isn't a corporate treasury — it's a wallet nobody can freeze.
