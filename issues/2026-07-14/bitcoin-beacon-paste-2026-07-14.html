African Shops Take Bitcoin, Get Paid in Cash

NAIROBI — In a Nairobi market, a fabric seller now takes bitcoin without ever holding a satoshi. The customer pays over the Lightning Network; a gateway converts it instantly; the shopkeeper’s account shows Kenyan shillings. The merchant never becomes a crypto business.

That model — accept bitcoin, settle in local currency — is spreading across Africa’s biggest markets. In Nigeria, a startup called Mular has processed about $500,000 letting customers pay in crypto while shops receive naira; BitSpenda runs the same rails in Ghana, Kenya and Nigeria; Machankura reaches feature phones over USSD. The pitch is cost: a Lightning-to-fiat checkout runs 0.5% to 1%, against 2.5% to 3.5% for card processors.

Why it matters: the biggest barrier to merchant bitcoin was asking shopkeepers to hold a volatile asset — settling in cash removes it.

Bitdeer’s 500-megawatt Jigmeling data center, co-owned by Bhutan’s sovereign investment arm, is scaling to capacity this year on pure hydropower.
