African Shops Take Bitcoin, Get Paid in Cash

A settle-in-local-currency layer is spreading across Africa’s markets — the merchant never has to hold a coin.

NAIROBI — In a Nairobi market, a fabric seller now takes bitcoin without ever holding a satoshi. The customer pays over the Lightning Network; a payment gateway converts it instantly; the shopkeeper’s account shows Kenyan shillings.

That model — accept bitcoin, settle in local currency — is spreading across Africa’s biggest markets. In Nigeria, a startup called Mular has processed about $500,000 letting customers pay in crypto while shops receive naira. BitSpenda runs the same rails in Ghana, Kenya and Nigeria; Machankura reaches feature phones over USSD; Fedi bundles it into one wallet for low-cost Androids.

The pitch is cost. A Lightning-to-fiat checkout runs 0.5% to 1%, against 2.5% to 3.5% for card processors — real money on a continent where cards barely reach.

Why it matters: the biggest barrier to merchant bitcoin was asking shopkeepers to hold a volatile asset; settling in cash removes it.

The Take

Turkey Taxes the Exit From the Lira

Turks moved roughly $200 billion through crypto last year, the world’s fourth-largest market — a number built not on speculation but on a lira that has lost more than half its value. When a currency melts, people reach for anything that holds.

Ankara’s answer is not to ban the exit but to toll it. Paying with crypto has been illegal since 2021; a June 30 deadline just forced exchanges to license or close; and a proposed 10% withholding tax would take a cut of the escape. Debase the money, then charge people to leave it.

Policy & Nation-States

JAKARTA. Indonesia is building a walled garden. On July 3 the exchange BTSE launched a licensed local venture under the country’s new Law No. 4, one of only a handful cleared by the regulator OJK — which shut 228 unlicensed platforms in the first five months of the year. For a nation of 280 million with tens of millions of crypto users, the door is open, but narrow. Full story →

Network & Mining

JIGMELING. The Himalayan kingdom’s state bitcoin mine is reaching full power. Bitdeer’s 500-megawatt Jigmeling data center, co-owned by Bhutan’s sovereign investment arm, is scaling to capacity this year on pure hydropower. Combined with existing sites, Bhutan now mines an estimated 11 to 12 bitcoin a day — real income for a country of under 800,000 that already holds more than a billion dollars in bitcoin. NEW YORK. Michael Saylor’s Strategy has started grading the banks. Its new Bitcoin Banking Adoption Index scores the sector at 32% overall — Fidelity leads at 71%, with Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan in the mid-40s. Money & Macro

BOGOTÁ. As the peso slides, Colombians are parking savings in crypto. About six million now use digital assets, and the country moved some $44 billion on-chain over the past year, among Latin America’s five largest markets. Most of that is dollar-pegged stablecoins; bitcoin is the smaller, issuer-less share — the part no bank or government can freeze. Full story →
