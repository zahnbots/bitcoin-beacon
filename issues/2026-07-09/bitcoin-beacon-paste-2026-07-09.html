Paraguay Earns More From Half as Many Bitcoin Miners

ASUNCIÓN — Paraguay's state utility ANDE expects a record $350 million in bitcoin-mining tariffs this year — up from $100 million two years ago — while its licensed miners fell from 71 to 41. The survivors are giants: four firms reserve 730 MW of hydro power, more than one Itaipú turbine produces. Multi-million-dollar deposits and higher rates cleared out the small farms; the tax authority chases gains at 10%; the first mining criminal case is in court in Villarrica.

Behind it all sits a date. Every mining contract expires December 31, 2027 — "non-negotiable," says ANDE, which wants the electricity back for the AI data centers President Peña is courting. New money arrives anyway: Korea's Bitplanet switched on machines at Paraguayan sites this month.

Why it matters: mining's edge is renting stranded power. Paraguay is the test of what happens when a richer tenant shows up.
