Bolivia’s First Bitcoin Merchant Is a Ceviche Shop

A cevichería in La Paz becomes the first shop in Bolivia to accept bitcoin — two years after the country banned it.

LA PAZ — The first bitcoin payment in Bolivia’s capital arrived not by decree but at a lunch counter. El Pulpo, a cevichería that has served Peruvian food in the Achumani district for nearly thirty years, this month became the first merchant in the country to accept bitcoin over the Lightning Network. The mechanism was mundane: a customer in a “Bitcoin Research” shirt walked the owner through the Blink wallet, a QR code went up by the register, and Bolivia’s circular-economy experiment had its first storefront.

Two years ago the transaction would have been illegal. Bolivia banned crypto in 2020; the central bank reversed course in June 2024, and volume has since reached roughly $14.8 billion. Most Bolivians fleeing a year-long dollar shortage have reached for dollar stablecoins — on July 11 the government confirmed it is drafting rules to recognize USDT as payment. El Pulpo chose the asset no company can freeze and no ministry can switch off, in a country that criminalized it 24 months ago.

Why it matters: no law made bitcoin legal tender in Bolivia. A ceviche shop just made it usable.

Bitcoin Traded Like a Tech Stock. That’s Fine.

During nine nights of U.S. strikes on Iran and a Strait of Hormuz shipping scare, bitcoin slid toward $63,700 with equities instead of spiking like gold. The “digital gold” crowd winced. They shouldn’t. That trade is a Wall Street story about who owns bitcoin today. Markets & Institutions

FRANKFURT. A new Deutsche Börse ETF, ticker MINE, gives European institutions their first UCITS-regulated exposure to bitcoin miners. It holds no bitcoin — only the shares of the firms that produce it — a workaround for the €26.3 trillion in pension and insurance money statutorily barred from the asset itself. Policy & Nation-States

WASHINGTON. The CLARITY Act would hand the CFTC primary authority over bitcoin’s spot market, sparing it the SEC’s securities regime. Its political logjam broke this week when Trump-aligned lawmakers accepted ethics provisions to peel off Democratic objections. The Senate Banking Committee has until the August recess to move it; prediction markets give it 43% by year-end. Full story →

SEOUL. South Korea’s finance ministry unveiled a second-half agenda on July 13: spot crypto ETFs for the first time, a tokenized government-bond pilot, and a won-stablecoin law before year-end.
