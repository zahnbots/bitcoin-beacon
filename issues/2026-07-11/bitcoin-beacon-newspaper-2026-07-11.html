Kazakhstan Cuts Taxes to Pull Bitcoin Onshore

The country that scattered its miners in 2021 now wants the hashrate — and the traders — back inside a licensed perimeter.

ASTANA — The country that chased out its bitcoin miners in the 2021 crackdown now wants them back. On July 8 President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev signed a decree that exempts income from digital-asset trades on licensed Kazakh platforms from personal income tax, lets miners burn otherwise-flared gas for power, and builds on a state crypto reserve the National Bank valued last autumn at $500 million to $1 billion.

The logic is jurisdictional. Kazakhstan was briefly the world’s #2 mining hub before blackouts and a ban scattered the rigs; the new rules try to pull that hashrate — and the traders who followed it offshore — back inside a taxable, licensed border.

Money & Macro

SAN SALVADOR. The reserve just passed 7,680 BTC, worth more than $510 million, still growing by roughly a coin a day — exactly what El Salvador’s $1.4 billion IMF program told it to stop. San Salvador calls the gains wallet consolidation, not purchases; on-chain trackers and Pete Rizzo count 170-plus BTC bought in 2026. The reconciliation, not the price, is what could freeze the Fund’s payouts. Full story →

Network & Mining

GERD, ETHIOPIA. Phoenix Group has brought 132 megawatts of mining online at the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam, with another 90 MW due this year — enough to push its own hashrate toward 13 EH/s. Ethiopia already runs roughly 2.6% of the global network on hydropower. The catch: the state utility has stopped issuing new mining permits, saying the grid is full. Full story →

Public Lightning capacity touched a record above 5,600 BTC this spring — roughly half a billion dollars across 41,000 channels — even as the count of public nodes kept falling. Monthly volume runs north of $1.1 billion. Capital is concentrating into fewer, larger, professionally run routing nodes. Whether that reads as maturity or as centralization is the open question.
